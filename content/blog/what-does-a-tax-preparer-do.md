Many people think of a tax preparer simply as someone who fills out forms. In reality, a skilled tax professional provides a range of services that go well beyond data entry. Understanding what a tax preparer actually does helps you make better use of their expertise and get more value from the relationship.

## Gathering and Organizing Your Financial Information

Before a single number goes on a form, a tax preparer works to understand your complete financial picture. They review your income documents — W-2s from employers, 1099s from clients or financial institutions, K-1s from partnerships or S corporations, and any other income records you have received.

They look at your deductions, including mortgage interest statements, charitable contribution records, medical expenses, and business expenses. They review prior year returns to identify carryforward items like capital losses or unused credits. A thorough preparer asks questions about life changes — marriage, divorce, a new child, a home purchase, a job change, the start or end of a business — that might affect your tax situation.

## Preparing Federal and State Returns

The core service is preparing accurate federal and state income tax returns. For individuals, this typically means a Form 1040 along with any applicable schedules. For business owners, it may include Schedule C for sole proprietors, or separate business returns for corporations, partnerships, or LLCs.

State returns vary significantly from state to state. Some states closely follow federal rules while others have their own deductions, credits, and filing requirements. A knowledgeable tax preparer understands the rules for your state and ensures you are not overpaying or making errors on your state return.

## Identifying Deductions and Credits

One of the most valuable things a tax preparer does is identify deductions and credits you might have missed on your own. Tax law is complex and changes regularly. A good preparer stays current on those changes and applies them to your situation.

Common areas where preparers find overlooked savings include home office deductions for self-employed individuals, vehicle mileage and expenses for business use, education credits for families with college students, retirement contribution deductions, energy efficiency credits for home improvements, and child and dependent care credits.

## Tax Planning Throughout the Year

The best tax preparers do not just look backward — they help you look forward. Tax planning involves analyzing your current situation and making decisions during the year that will reduce your tax bill. This might include timing income and deductions strategically, making additional retirement contributions before year end, harvesting investment losses to offset gains, or adjusting your withholding to avoid underpayment penalties.

Some preparers offer mid-year check-ins or are available to answer questions throughout the year, not just during tax season. This ongoing relationship can be enormously valuable when you face a major financial decision.

## Filing and Communicating With the IRS

Tax preparers handle the actual filing of your returns, whether electronically or by mail. They obtain your signature, apply their own PTIN, and submit the return. If you are owed a refund, they confirm your banking information for direct deposit. If you owe money, they help you understand your payment options.

If the IRS contacts you about your return, a credentialed tax preparer — particularly a CPA, Enrolled Agent, or tax attorney — can respond on your behalf. They understand IRS procedures and can communicate with the agency in ways that protect your interests.

## Representing You in an Audit

If your return is selected for examination, a qualified tax preparer can represent you before the IRS. This means gathering the documentation needed to support your return, communicating with the IRS examiner, and advocating for the correct tax treatment of items on your return.

Having professional representation during an audit can significantly reduce stress and improve outcomes. An experienced preparer knows what the IRS is looking for and how to respond effectively.

## Record Keeping Guidance

A good tax preparer will tell you what records to keep and for how long. The IRS generally has three years to audit a return, but that window extends to six years if you underreport income by more than 25 percent, and there is no statute of limitations on fraud. Knowing which records to keep — and which you can safely discard — helps you stay organized and protected.
